# Presentation Modes

Choose the mode by communication job rather than industry. Use the rules as a starting point and respect an existing template.

## Recurring status or operational review

- Keep section order and core layouts stable across periods.
- Lead with changes, exceptions, risks, decisions needed, and next actions.
- Show comparisons against plan or prior period, not isolated current values.
- Allow moderate density so the deck remains useful after the meeting.

## Decision or approval document

- Structure the story as context, issue, options, criteria, recommendation, impact, risks, and requested decision.
- Make alternatives comparable and disclose assumptions.
- Put the decision request and owner/date in explicit language.
- Include detail in an appendix without hiding material risks.

## Proposal or persuasive presentation

- Establish the audience problem before presenting the solution.
- Connect benefits to evidence, feasibility, cost, and next steps.
- Use a confident visual hierarchy without exaggerating claims.
- End with a concrete action, commitment, or choice.

## Research, analysis, or management report

- State scope, sources, period, definitions, and limitations.
- Distinguish observation, interpretation, and recommendation.
- Use charts for evidence and annotations for the key insight.
- Keep methodology and supporting tables accessible in the appendix.

## Training, manual, or reference material

- Organize around learning objectives or tasks.
- Use one concept or action per step, consistent terminology, numbered procedures, and annotated screenshots.
- Optimize for later self-service reading as well as live delivery.
- Repeat navigation and section cues when the deck is long.

## Sales, company introduction, or external communication

- Apply brand identity consistently and use credible, relevant visuals.
- Translate internal features into audience value and proof.
- Minimize internal jargon and disclose evidence sources.
- Balance memorability with enough substance to earn trust.

## Speech, seminar, or large-screen event

- Use large type, strong visual anchors, and low on-screen density.
- Let the speaker carry detail; avoid long tables and paragraphs.
- Design for viewing distance, room contrast, and pacing.
- Provide separate notes or handouts if the material must stand alone.

## Japanese internal corporate document

- Permit higher information density when the deck doubles as a circulated document.
- Use disciplined grids, visible premises, source notes, risks, and approval points to keep dense slides navigable.
- Prefer clarity and traceability over decorative minimalism.
- Do not use small text as the only solution to density; split the slide or move evidence to an appendix when needed.
